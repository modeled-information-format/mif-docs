# Butler's Restraint: Unreliable Narration in Kazuo Ishiguro's *The Remains of the Day*

## Introduction

Kazuo Ishiguro's *The Remains of the Day* (1989) is narrated by Stevens, a
butler whose account of his own life the novel steadily undermines. This essay
argues that Stevens's unreliability is not a lapse of honesty but a disciplined
performance of professional dignity, and that the gap between what Stevens
reports and what the reader is invited to infer is the novel's central formal
achievement.[^1]

## Dignity as Rhetorical Armor

Stevens repeatedly frames emotionally charged events — his father's death, Miss
Kenton's departure — in the flattened vocabulary of service. Wayne Booth's
concept of the unreliable narrator, one whose values or reporting diverge from
the implied author's, supplies the analytic frame: the reader senses a distance
between Stevens's account and the events themselves precisely because his
diction never varies with the stakes of what he describes.[^2] The evidence is
cumulative rather than singular — a repeated flatness of tone in scenes the
plot marks as pivotal.

## Free Indirect Access and Its Withholding

Where much modernist fiction opens a gap between narration and consciousness
through free indirect discourse, Ishiguro largely withholds that access:
Stevens narrates in first person but almost never reports his own feeling
directly, only his professional judgment of a situation. James Wood's account
of how free indirect style lets a text register a character's inner life while
maintaining narratorial distance is useful by contrast — Stevens's narration
refuses that opening even as the reader is positioned to supply the feeling
Stevens omits.[^3] The interpretation this section advances is that the
omission itself is the evidence: what Stevens does not say marks the scenes
that matter most.

## Conclusion

Stevens's unreliability is not incidental but constitutive: the novel's
meaning is produced in the space between his restrained report and the reader's
reconstruction of what that restraint costs him. Reading *The Remains of the
Day* as a study in disciplined self-suppression, rather than as a simple case
of a narrator who "gets it wrong," better accounts for the novel's sustained
formal control.
