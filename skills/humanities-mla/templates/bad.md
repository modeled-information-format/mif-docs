## Introduction

Modern fiction repeatedly stages technology as an alienating force that
estranges characters from their own labor and even their own bodies. This
essay argues that the recurring figure of the machine in twentieth-century
novels dramatizes a loss of selfhood that criticism has not fully named.

## The Machine as Estrangement

Critics have long noted that industrial technology reshapes the worker's
relationship to their own output (Marcuse 45). The novel extends this claim
to the level of interiority: characters described in mechanical terms lose
access to their own interior monologue, becoming observers of their own
actions rather than agents of them.

## The Cyborg as Counter-Figure

Some readings treat the human-machine boundary as productive rather than
alienating, a site where new forms of agency become possible (Haraway 12).

## Conclusion

The machine in modern fiction is therefore best read as this essay's
introduction claimed: an alienating figure with no serious counter-reading
worth taking seriously.
