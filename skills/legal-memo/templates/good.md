# Legal Memorandum: Hendricks Non-Compete / Client-List Question

To: Supervising Partner
From: Associate, Litigation Group
Date: 2026-06-30
Re: Whether Hendricks' use of a client list drawn from public trade directories breaches her non-compete

## Question Presented

Under the governing non-compete and trade-secret doctrine, does a former
account manager who contacts former clients using a list compiled entirely
from publicly available industry trade directories breach a covenant not to
solicit clients using confidential information?

## Brief Answer

Likely no. A client list assembled solely from public trade directories is
not a trade secret, because it lacks the independent economic value from
secrecy that trade-secret protection requires. The non-solicitation covenant
is enforceable only to the extent it protects a legitimate business interest
such as genuine confidential information or account-specific relationships;
absent that, the covenant cannot reach solicitation built on public-source
data alone.

## Statement of Facts

Hendricks worked as an account manager at Meridian Supply Co. for four years
under an employment agreement containing a two-year non-solicitation covenant
covering "clients with whom Hendricks had material contact." After resigning,
Hendricks compiled a list of industrial-parts buyers using only two publicly
available trade directories and began contacting them on behalf of a
competitor. Meridian has not identified any pricing terms, account history,
or other non-public information that Hendricks retained or used.

## Discussion

### Issue A: Is the client list a protectable trade secret?

**Issue.** Does a customer list assembled entirely from public trade
directories qualify as a trade secret under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act?

**Rule.** The Uniform Trade Secrets Act defines a trade secret as information
that derives independent economic value from not being generally known and is
the subject of reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy. Unif. Trade
Secrets Act § 1(4) (Unif. Law Comm'n 1985). The Restatement similarly
recognizes that a customer list compiled from public sources ordinarily does
not qualify, because the value comes from the compilation effort at most, not
from secrecy. Restatement (Third) of Unfair Competition § 39 cmt. f (Am. Law
Inst. 1995).

**Application.** Meridian has not shown that the list contains anything
beyond what the two public directories already disclose — no pricing history,
no account-specific terms, no data Hendricks would have needed her employment
to learn. Meridian's best counter-argument is that Hendricks' four years of
material contact let her curate the list more efficiently than a stranger
could; that argument speaks to compilation effort, not secrecy, and the
Restatement treats compilation effort alone as insufficient. § 39 cmt. f.

**Conclusion.** The list is not a trade secret; Meridian's trade-secret theory
is unlikely to succeed on these facts.

### Issue B: Is the non-solicitation covenant enforceable against this conduct?

**Issue.** Does the covenant reach solicitation conducted with information
that is not confidential?

**Rule.** A covenant not to solicit is enforceable only to the extent
necessary to protect a legitimate business interest; it may not be used to
suppress ordinary competition unmoored from confidential information or
unique client relationships. BDO Seidman v. Hirshberg, 93 N.Y.2d 382, 712
N.E.2d 1220 (1999).

**Application.** BDO Seidman held that a covenant could not bar an accountant
from serving clients he developed no unique relationship with beyond ordinary
service delivery, where the employer showed no protectable interest beyond
generic client contact. Here, Meridian's showing is even thinner: it points
to no confidential pricing or account data at all, only public-directory
information Hendricks could have obtained without ever working at Meridian.

**Conclusion.** The covenant, applied to this conduct, is unlikely to be
enforced; Meridian has not identified the kind of protectable interest BDO
Seidman requires.

## Conclusion

Meridian's claims against Hendricks are unlikely to succeed on the facts
presented. Both the trade-secret and non-solicitation theories depend on
confidential information Meridian has not shown exists. Recommend advising
the client that pursuing an injunction on this record carries a low
probability of success, and that any further action should focus first on
determining whether Hendricks retained any non-public account data.
